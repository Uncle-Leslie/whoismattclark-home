Strong For Carrie took fourteen months to write and record, and something like ten years to materialize.  In early June of 1998 (looking for a way out, I'm sure, as songwriters often are) I sat down to fall apart and instead penned "Out There Now."  It came as a shock to me...I didn't think I was capable of that level of self-comprehension just then, and something shifted noticeably.  "A Still Rising" followed, after more than a month of renewed exploration, and it stands as a potent indicator, I think, of those boons that might be waiting for you, if you're lucky enough to get to point B. (You'll just have to trust me on this one...) Though I didn't know it, the record had bookends now -- that elusive dual-terminus of a story arc.
    Returning to school that autumn, I felt largely healed. Time passes, a mind wanders, to quote my favorite lyricist. From this steadier ground came "Better Tricks," a pretty massive expansion on the optimism of "Still Rising."  The song contained a reference to the fictitious girl who would eventully become Carrie, and I suppose it was the impetus, the first flicker of a new album's gestation.  It paved the way for "Live to See," whose retrospective nature got me thinking hard about this nameless, faceless figure, gathering the pieces and so forth. I tried to pin her down as a variation on Springsteen's Mary in the verses of "When I Stood with Mary" and "Worn Well," but the pivotal moment, looking back, was "The Later Show," a tune I literally dreamed into existence.  In it I wrote of being "between my first love's house and home," and suddenly Mary made sense to
me.  I tucked her away and headed for the new year.
    By Christmas I'd written "Decent Days" at the request of a good friend, really pushing that envelope and tumbling all over myself to make ends meet -- rock and roll plus introspection...the famous strangers.  "Thanksgiving" closed 1998 on an appropriate note: missing the Grand Illusion but little else. In early January I commissioned myself to write "Amethyst" as a personal birthday present, just to prove I could write a love song with no ties to my own legendary misadventures (time would reatly diminish my success on this one...).  February was insane, explosive, opening with "Page Two," barreling into "Names in Shade" and "Some of Mine (Saints and Babies)" over a two-day period, and slamming shut with "Stranger Music."  "Page Two" was a love letter, a definite change of pace, but "Names in Shade" was the breakthrough.  It was a nod to my closest friends and a very specific era of our lives -- young, naive, spinning out of control...we were all, in our ways, hunting for a romantic ideal, a culmination of all that adolescent yearning.  We were looking for Carrie.  "Stranger Music" celebrated this revelation on my part, and then, as they say, I rested.
    Strong For Carrie went into production in late May of 1999 under the guise of EKB VII. Eventually it became clear that I was going to be alone on this one, and I had to do some radical rethinking: I put together the most representative list of the past year's work, changed a few "Mary"s to "Carrie"s, broke the movements with a pair of instrumentals, and closed my eyes as tightly as possible.  Recording wrapped up in August, and post-production has stretched into mid-September as I write this.  Best I can tell, the album described here is the purest photograph I could manage of one boy's heaviest reconciliation -- early hopes and later truths.  As always, I hope the confines of my stories aren't too restrictive, that there's plenty of room left in there. I'm trusting that time will tell, as usual, and until then...
